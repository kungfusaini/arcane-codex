Metamorphosis is a fascinating study into human nature. Our protagonist Gregor wakes up transformed into an insect and the novel details his family life after the fact, exploring the question of whether our relationships can ever be truly selfless. 

Note:
> I encourage you to read the book before this codex entry. I will be divulging major plot points (and it's a short read anyways)!

Do human beings only form relationships based on mutual gain? Over the course of the book, we see how Gregor's family relationships unravel as they care for him in his new, incomprehensible, state. Although his parents are initially too shocked with his transformation to even face him, his sister takes the effort to help him as best she can. She cares for her brother. Not only does she try to discover what food his new form will accept, but she takes responsibility for looking after her brother when no one else can. But she becomes resentful of this position. Even though Gregor is internally grateful for her service, he is not capable of expressing his gratitude. Without this feedback, it seems the duty of helping a family member becomes a thankless burden, which everyone becomes annoyed with. They are performing a service out of respect to his former position, not for him now... It's interesting to explore the idea that maybe if Gregor was able to express his gratitude, his family would provide his care with joy. 

I also think that the family would be more accepting of their position if the outside world could comprehend their situation. Imagine if Gregor was simply paralysed rather than being transformed. Although I can imagine that the family might eventually grow tired of their duties, it's more difficult to see them wanting to cast him out of the house. When someone is paralysed, people are sympathetic, come to visit, and assist when they can. The fact that Gregor's condition needs to be kept a secret puts additional stress on the family. Perhaps love is not the only emotion which families draw upon to care for the sick, but also the social shame that would come with neglecting a family member in need.

Human beings are inherently accepting of their own, and put lightly, {{< emph >}}cautious of outsiders{{< /emph >}}. As soon as we stop relating to something, we are able to treat it as subhuman. A shift occurs during the last few pages of the novel. As soon as the family stops referring to Gregor by his name, deciding just to call him "it", they start making plans to remove him from the home. Even though this change in language was probably unconscious on the part of the family, it demonstrates this dehumanising effect perfectly. We see this in war and news coverage all the time, where there are often bids to show the opposing side as savages, rather than complex human beings, whose motives might be just as valid as our own. We shield ourselves from the moral complexities of harming or shunning our fellow man by taking away their humanity. 

The family is saved from the difficulty of outing Gregor by his death. What stood out for me was the immediate and immense relief they felt after his demise. They finally feel like they can move onto a new life. You might feel this is selfish of them, to be happy at the death of a member, but it's still an understandable reaction. They have become overworked and depressed, caring for someone they know will not get better, and cannot even express themselves. I have heard of similar feelings from people who have looked after disabled children for example. There lives have become so consumed in the care of another that it feels like a prison. It's a terrible situation to find oneself in, but it does prove the point that relationships are only maintained if they are aligned with the lifestyle that one wishes to live. Some people have a greater capacity for care than others, and might be able to look after Gregor better than his family did. I believe this is because they are happy to do so, or feel very strongly that it's their duty. I am lucky to know some such people. If one is living a life they hate, be it always looking after another, or even something like living with a spouse they resent, it's clear to see that relief is a natural emotion that would arise once the situation comes to a close. 


My final thought on the novel was that nothing surprising happened. I mean this as the biggest compliment to the author. Kafka was able to present human emotions and reactions in a way that's not only logical and believable, but scarily relatable. It holds a dark mirror in front of us and asks: {{< emph >}}"Would you do any different?"{{< /emph >}}
